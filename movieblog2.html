By: Vanessa Comulada

Description of Chosen Media

In an ever-globalized and culturally intermingled world, we are living in a time when media plays a significant role in ameliorating cultural gaps. Spirited Away, directed by Hayao Miyazaki and produced by Studio Ghibli, represents a world of exceptional animation while offering viewers a glimpse into Japanese culture and a storyline that includes themes of various natures. The story centers around a young girl named Chihiro who finds herself lost in a supernatural spirit world where she must endure her personal development while experiencing the challenges of acculturation through this adventure. Furthermore, Spirited Away adequately demonstrates Japanese beliefs, customs, and values, providing a rich case study for intercultural communication comprehension. The following project will examine the cultural setting surrounding Spirited Away, and its connection to intercultural themes, and I will provide a personal reflection on how the movie demonstrates the nature of empathy, adaptation, and identity during experiences of acculturation.

Spirited Away is a 2001 Japanese animated fantasy film directed by Hayao Miyazaki and produced by Studio Ghibli. The film tells the story of Chihiro, a 10-year-old girl who finds herself trapped in a hidden spirit world where her parents become pigs. To survive and recover her parents, she is forced to work in a bathhouse for spirits run by a powerful witch (Yubaba). As the story progresses, Chihiro loses her name, encounters challenges, and grows in resilience, kindness, and self-awareness. At its heart, the film is also a coming-of-age story, and Chihiro’s physical and emotional development speaks to broader themes of identity and change and the dichotomy of tradition versus modernity. The world is richly textured with traditional Japanese architecture, folklore, and spiritual influences. Characters such as No-Face, Haku, and Yubaba, each signify a specific aspect of human nature or societal problem (greed, troubled memory, exploitation). Coupled with picturesque visuals, a hauntingly beautiful soundtrack by Joe Hisaishi, and a narrative that appeals to children and adults alike, Spirited Away captured the cultural imagination and became a worldwide phenomenon. It remains Japan's highest-grossing film (until a recent rival), and won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature Film.

Relevance to Intercultural Communication

One of the most appealing elements of Spirited Away is the film's deep grounding in Japanese culture, allowing for rich text for intercultural communication. Embodying Shinto beliefs, we see kami (spirits), ritual cleaning/purification methods, and respect for nature in play. The bathhouse itself is not just an occupational site but is a cultural concept, an apparent reference to Japan's historical communal bathhouse and spaces for both cleansing and social interaction. Being clean and spiritually clean is a constant theme we see (and hear at a narrative sound level) in the film - as demonstrated in the scene where Chihiro helps cleanse the river spirit of their pollution. It alludes to the importance of customs related to cleansing and spiritual purity in Shinto beliefs. The significance of names also reflects Japanese cultural values. Losing one's name, as Chihiro's name gets taken by Yubaba and renamed "Sen," represents a loss of one's identity and power - historically rooted concerns of Japanese members in society as it relates to conformity and one's position within a group. Getting her real name back is a very important part of Chihiro reframing her selfhood, reading as a metaphor for navigating a new culture without taking roots away from themselves.

The movie also creates a bridge for audiences between Eastern and Western audiences. While its premise and culture are Japanese, the emotional experiences of fear, bravery, and development are emotionally human and relatable regardless of culture. Its successful reception exemplifies that there are culturally specific stories that can connect people across cultural differences through human experience. This film is a beautiful example of intercultural communication in a way that is understated and enjoyable. In the considered process of adaptation into English, alongside Disney, and the Miyazaki team, there were decisions made to exchange background information and concepts for Western viewer understanding. This also involved adaptations that reveal the limitations of translation to contextualize cultural understandings. Similarly, negotiating decisions around cultural choices, or experiences, can aid the fluid and enriching process of being involved in intermedia cultural fluidity. Chihiro's process can be likened to metaphorical experiences, and entering new cultures. Chihiro begins her journey as a frightened and uninvited participant. As she moves across the line of participation through the process of being at her own pace and from respect, work, and humility she learns the customs, earns the trust, and ultimately finds her way in the culture. This is akin to negotiating cultural experiences in social contexts, where participants and actors must learn new cultural norms while trying to manage their own cultural identities. Chihiro's relationships with Haku, Lin, and even No-Face reveal the complexity of relating and communicating with multicultural contexts, the negotiations of language, gestures, and/or values, and the greater layer of working towards understanding each other.

Analysis and Personal Take

Spirited Away, spoke to me as a beautiful film, and stunning visually but it was also a lesson in connection and cultural empathy as an anthropological human awareness. I was able to relate Chihiro's process of development in a context that feels like an unnecessary situational adjustment. As a personal reflection, I have felt that sense of transitioning every time I began new schooling, or flew over the water and worked with people from various cultural backgrounds. I could witness Chihiro's journey evolve as she experienced fear, and confusion, through her processes with experience in any new context or cultural adjustment. Chihiro's process reminds me that if you were there with respect, and open to processes of work, it can also help to situate human behaviors for cultural adjustment and/or experience.

Visually, it is astounding. I recognized that each frame was carefully constructed, saturated with cultural meanings—the kind of cultural meanings that the unaware viewer may not even notice is occurring—but it was enough to draw me in and want to learn about Japanese culture. Everything, even the specific bathhouse architecture, and food stalls, transported me into a world that felt unfamiliar in clear ways—but also warm and inviting. The music was equally impressive. Joe Hisaishi created a score of traditional Japanese instruments and classical instrumentation that added emotional depth to the film without words or lyrics. What I found most significant, however, is how the film encourages the viewer to view a different perspective. It doesn't go out of its way to convey each line of dialogue or translate everything for the Western view, and I feel this is powerful. Rather than a Westernized or simplified approach to storytelling, Miyazaki asks the viewer to engage and invest in their thinking—recognize a cultural difference, see the difference, honor the difference—so it is not so much judgment, but appreciation. Because that is exactly what Spirit Away is, rather than an entertainment film it is a cultural bridge, or more modestly so.

Summary

Spirited Away is such a wonderful example of a cultural bridge. The abundance of Japanese spiritual ideals - with the symbolic use of language and naming of a way to elicit universal feelings in the emotional involvement in the film is impressive. It facilitates seeing the world through a cultural lens when it does not seem to matter or seem necessary as an engagement for the actors and audience to engage in thinking around identity, empathy, and adaptation—when it does matter. Rather, it has themes that could facilitate and apply cross-culturally, which is why it is such a powerful medium for connecting to investigating communication across cultures. For me, it created a greater understanding of the sharing and appreciation of Japanese culture and a renewed attunement to the value of humility and curiosity when engaging in cross-cultural experiences. In a time when it is more important than ever that our care for understanding and respect for each other is sustained, Spirited Away continues to astonish us through the historical lens of a beautifully animated spirit world.
